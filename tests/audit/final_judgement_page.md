The exact core is finite-dimensional positive-cone / local quadratic observation geometry:

- Canonical lift, hidden projector, tower composition, and Schur formulas.

- Support-stratum transport, finite restart maps, kernel Schur-jet support-event classification, and sampled interval diagnostics with their stated sample-only boundary.

## What Is Only Exact In Gaussian Law Mode?

Full observed laws and closed-form probabilistic comparisons are exact only in Gaussian mode:

- Gaussian equilibrium-shadow and noisy-channel formulas;
- Gaussian common gluing, Bell/temporal sign-shadow formulas, and covariance-completion criteria;

## What Survives Robustly Outside Gaussianity?

The positive-cone and Fisher/Hessian quotient geometry survives exactly when the input is a valid supplied local SPD Hessian/Fisher object. Some full-law identities in the wider paper stack also survive beyond Gaussianity, but they are separate identities: KL chain rule, regular Fisher quotient descent, total covariance, Hilbert projection, and quadratic CME/CLE shadows with a sharp cubic boundary.

Two broader exact law sectors should now be separated from merely empirical survival:

- fixed-shape quadratic tilts, where the symmetric KL along precision rays is exactly controlled by the variance of the quadratic statistic;
- fixed-precision affine-hidden fibres, where hidden marginalisation gives `A(v)-1/2 J(v)^T D^{-1}J(v)+const` and staged elimination is Schur-associative.
- white or aligned rank-one differential-correlation covariance sectors, where Sherman-Morrison gives a one-channel hidden-gap formula and the Fisher ceiling exactly.

Empirically, elliptic or weak higher-order families can also preserve the quadratic observer ranking, but that is approximate family-specific survival unless the law is inside a named exact sector.

## What Needs New Theory?

Full non-Gaussian observation needs higher-order and support-aware law data:

- cubic hidden-visible coupling;
- quartic/tail and cumulant structure;
- mixture and branch data;
- hard support, atoms, and threshold events;
- non-Gaussian gluing beyond covariance;
- observer-selection objectives that combine local Fisher geometry with higher-order law divergence.
- fibre-volume terms such as `1/2 log det D(v)` when hidden precision depends on the visible variable.
- coloured-background differential correlations, where a rank-one covariance perturbation becomes a two-rank hidden-gap update unless the relevant visible directions align.
- ensembles of local quadratic geometries, where tail-sensitive distributions of clocks and hidden loads cannot be replaced by a single averaged Hessian.
- noncommuting closure frontiers, because generic symmetric perturbation families need not admit exact intermediate-rank closure observers.

The stress tests show that this is a real frontier: two laws can share the same Hessian while visible laws differ; observer rankings can reverse; quadratic closure can fail at the full-law level.

## Does The README Clarification Undersell Or Oversell?

It is correct and does not undersell the module. It should be read strictly:

> outside exact Gaussian law mode, `nomogeo` is exact for supplied local quadratic Hessian or Fisher geometry, not for full non-Gaussian laws.

The only tightening I would suggest is interpretive: "Fisher geometry" means a genuinely supplied local Fisher metric whose quotient is the object of study. It should not be read as a promise that the module computes the observed Fisher information or full pushed-forward law for arbitrary non-Gaussian observation channels.

## Plain Answer

The project has found the right backbone. It is not merely a Gaussian exact theory. It is an exact local quadratic observation geometry, with Gaussian probability as the first fully solved full-law sector and some narrower exact non-Gaussian or covariance/Fisher sectors now visible. The current danger is not that the core is wrong; it is that branch, support, closure, and observer-selection verdicts can become unstable if local quadratic exactness is silently promoted to generic full non-Gaussian law exactness.
