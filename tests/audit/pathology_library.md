# Non-Gaussian Pathology Library

The examples below are explicit breakpoints where local quadratic geometry and full visible law verdicts separate. Numerical values are generated by `audit/non_gaussian_stress_tests.py` and `audit/weakpoint_research_numerics.py`.

## P1. Same Hessian, Heavy Tails

Construction: compare `N(0,1)` to a Student `t_5` law with scale `sqrt(6/5)`. The Student scale is chosen so that `-d^2 log p(0)=1`, matching the Gaussian local Hessian exactly.

Quadratic prediction: collapse. The local visible precision is `H=1` for both laws, so every `Phi_C`, `V`, `Q`, hidden-load object built only from that Hessian agrees.

Full visible law: the tails differ. The script gives symmetric KL `0.255837` and squared Hellinger `0.0195813`.

Why it matters: full-law visible distinguishability can be nonzero when the entire quadratic layer is identical.

## P2. Observer Ranking Reversal

Construction: in a two-coordinate product law, let the `x` coordinate change from `N(0,1)` to `N(0, 1/1.08)`, while the `y` coordinate keeps Hessian `1` but changes from `exp(-y^2/2)` to `exp(-y^2/2 - 0.35 y^4)` after normalization.

Quadratic prediction: the `x` observer wins because `Delta_H=(0.08,0)` and the `y` Hessian perturbation is zero.

Full visible law: the `y` observer wins. The script gives symmetric KL `0.00296296` for `x` and `0.904204` for `y`, a ratio about `305.17`.

Why it matters: quadratic observer selection can be decisively wrong if higher-order law shape is a task-relevant signal.

## P3. Quadratic Closure With Cubic Hidden-Visible Leakage

Construction: use the two-dimensional density

```text
p_t(x,z) ∝ exp[-0.5(x^2+z^2) - alpha(x^4+z^4) - t x z^2],
alpha = 0.15.
```

The cubic hidden-visible term has zero Hessian contribution at the mode. The comparison uses `t=0` and `t=0.28`.

Quadratic prediction: no visible response and no closure leakage at order two. The local Hessian at the mode is unchanged, so the local quadratic layer sees collapse.

Full visible law: integrating out `z` changes the marginal law of `x`. The script gives visible marginal symmetric KL `0.0139792`, squared Hellinger `0.00174601`, and mean shift from `0` to `-0.087432`.

Why it matters: `Q=0` is an exact order-two closure criterion, not a guarantee of full non-Gaussian marginal closure.

## P4. Matched Hessian With Remote Branches

Construction: compare `N(0,1)` to a symmetric mixture

```text
(1-eps) N(0, s0^2) + eps/2 N(5, 0.35^2) + eps/2 N(-5, 0.35^2),
eps = 0.05,
```

where `s0` is calibrated so that the Hessian at `0` is exactly `1` (the calibration returns `s0≈1` because the remote branch is tiny at the mode).

Quadratic prediction: central-mode collapse.

Full visible law: threshold/event observers see the remote branch. The probability of `|x|>3` rises from `0.0026998` to `0.0525648`, and symmetric KL is `0.473808`.

Why it matters: branch or event structure can be invisible to local Hessian geometry.

## P5. Same Interior Hessian, Different Support

Construction: compare `N(0,1)` to `N(0,1)` conditioned on `x>=-2`. Around the interior point `0`, the log-density Hessian is exactly the same as the Gaussian Hessian.

Quadratic prediction: same local quadratic precision `H=1` at `0`.

Full visible law: support differs. The truncated law assigns probability `0` to `x<-2`; the Gaussian assigns probability `0.0227501`. `KL(gaussian || truncated)` is infinite, while `KL(truncated || gaussian)=0.0230129`.

Why it matters: the module's support strata are ranks of supplied PSD matrices, not hard probability supports or boundary singularities of arbitrary laws.

## P6. Variable Hidden Precision Fibre-Volume Term

Construction: use a visible-dependent hidden Gaussian fibre

```text
p(v,h) proportional to exp(-A(v)-1/2 D(v) h^2-J(v)h).
```

In the hostile case set `A=0`, `J=0`, and `D(v)=exp(2v)` on a bounded interval.

Quadratic / variational prediction: hidden minimisation is flat, so no visible branch is selected.

Full visible law: exact marginalisation gives

```text
S_vis(v)=1/2 log D(v)+const = v+const.
```

The exact law selects the smallest allowed `v`; the variational quotient cannot distinguish any `v`. The weakpoint numerics measured a missing log-determinant range of `0.935` and exact argmin at the interval boundary.

Why it matters: fixed hidden precision is not cosmetic in the affine-hidden exact sector. Once `D=D(v)`, the exact law is still integrable but belongs to an entropic fibre-volume sector, not the fixed-precision minimisation/marginalisation coincidence theorem.

## P7. Quadratic Branch Tie, Fibre-Cumulant Separation

Construction: compare two candidate branches with the same local quadratic datum. Perturb one branch by the hidden-visible cubic term `epsilon x z^2` under a quartic product baseline and leave the other unperturbed.

Quadratic prediction: branch gap `0`; no second-order visible response.

Full visible law: the perturbed branch separates immediately through the first fibre cumulant. At `epsilon=0.18`, the weakpoint numerics give visible symmetric KL `0.0027934`, visible mean shift `-0.0350396`, and second-order symmetric-KL prediction `0.0027580`.

Why it matters: a full-law branch verdict can change even when the quadratic branch selector reports a tie.

## P8. One-Channel Differential-Correlation Claim in a Coloured Background

Construction: use a covariance perturbation

```text
Sigma_eps = Sigma_0 + eps f f^T
```

with a generic SPD background `Sigma_0`. Let `H_0=Sigma_0^{-1}`, `Phi_0=(Sigma_0)_{VV}^{-1}`, `u_V=(H_0 f)_V`, and `w_V=Phi_0 f_V`.

Quadratic prediction if one overimports the white-sector result: the differential correlation should produce a single visible leakage channel aligned with `f_V`.

Actual quadratic geometry: the hidden-gap increment is

```text
Delta R =
  eps/(1+eps f_V^T Phi_0 f_V) * w_V w_V^T
  - eps/(1+eps f^T H_0 f) * u_V u_V^T.
```

It has rank at most two and is rank one only when `u_V` and `w_V` are collinear. The follow-up script found a rank-two example with formula error `1.18e-16` and alignment `0.809`.

Why it matters: a true observation-design conclusion can split into two channels once background covariance is coloured. The Claude-note statement is valid in its toy sector, not as a generic theorem.

## P9. Generic Noncommuting Closure Obstruction

Construction: take `D_1=diag(1,...,n)` and a generic symmetric `D_2`. For rank `m`, exact closure means the observer subspace is invariant under both `D_1` and `D_2`.

Quadratic prediction if one overimports the commuting examples: a closure-adapted observer with `eta=0` should be available at intermediate ranks.

Actual quadratic geometry: since `D_1` has simple spectrum, every `D_1`-invariant subspace is a coordinate eigenspan. Exact common closure then requires a zero cross-block of `D_2` for one coordinate subset, a measure-zero condition. The follow-up script used `n=6`, `m=2`, commutator norm `6.80`, and smallest coordinate cross-block certificate `1.113`; exact common rank-2 closure was absent.

Why it matters: exact closure is an invariant-subspace theorem, not a generic observer-design guarantee. Noncommuting families need Pareto or approximate frontier optimisation, not silent exact-closure language.

## P10. Single Averaged Hessian Misses Local-Geometry Tails

Construction: sample local coupling strengths from two different distributions with the same mean and push each local Hessian through the exact clock map.

Quadratic prediction if reduced to one representative Hessian: using only the mean coupling gives a single clock value.

Actual ensemble geometry: in the follow-up script both ensembles had mean coupling `0.13315`, while the clock at the mean was `0.02363`. The mean clocks were `0.03734` and `0.03455`, and the 99th percentile clocks were `0.38274` and `0.48870`.

Why it matters: `tau(E H_local)` is not `E tau(H_local)`, and tail-sensitive observational structure can live in the distribution of local quadratic objects. This motivates an ensemble layer; it is not captured by one Hessian.
